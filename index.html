Is the Michelin Star Rankings Biased?

By Benjamin Goundry

Introduction: https://guide.michelin.com/th/en/to-the-stars-and-beyond-th

The Michelin Star rating system has existed for decades as a means of rating restaurants around the world. It is generally considered one of the most significant restaurant ranking systems in the world, but it is not without its flaws. Many articles have been written about the perceived bias of the rating system, in particular how it seems to favor French and Japanese cuisine and is very unobjective in its analysis. Michelin rankings are incredibly important to chefs and the pressure surrounding them has even contributed to chefs committing suicide.

https://www.scmp.com/lifestyle/food-drink/article/2183133/criticism-controversies-suicides-brief-history-michelin-guide

https://frenchly.us/is-the-michelin-guide-prejudiced/

Thus I believe it is an incredibly important subject to analyze. Michelin Stars have significant impacts of business, tourism, and the economics of areas with more of them and if they are not distributed in an equitable and unprejudiced way, it could have significant negative impacts on marginalized communites and countries.

My Hypothesises:

Based on my research, I have several hypothesis regarding the Michelin Star rating system. First, I believe that I will discover that Michelin does tend to give more stars to restaurants serving French and Japanese cuisine, as well as restaurants that are more expensive. I believe that restaurants in Western countries will be given more stars, both overall and per capita, compared to non-Western countries. I believe that areas with higher per capita car ownership will have more Michelin stars (I will talk more about why later). And finally, I believe a restaurant's star rating will likely be able to be reliably predicted by a machine learning algorithm because of these biases.
